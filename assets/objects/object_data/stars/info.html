A Work that is dying, its intricacies blurring and ribboning, is a sadder thing than losing one's child, I imagine. I do. I remember the day that Daddy sped off in the sidecar. It was not June, and so the rock-rose was bloody in the clints. Mummy and I followed in the wagon, across the virgate and down to the port, and watched him standing, on the deck of Mummy's ferry, shouting that he would 'knock up some Boche ponces', or some equally stupid Babel, translated on the wind.

His sergeant brought back his wedding ring before Guy Fawkes Night. As Norris served him tea, he told us how twenty privates had tried to steal it from his pocket, as he zigged and zagged his way back to England through the trenches. I can see it so very clearly, the herringbone of his trousers, and the little cardboard box perched atop them. I loved that comparison; it looked a little like our house, stood in fertile fields.

The sergeant opened the box, and took out, very carefully, twenty fingers, and laid them on the Pembroke table. Of course, none of them were Daddy's, and so we shooed the sergeant from our life. We fed the fingers to the pigs.

No matter how much I might remember of this Work, day after day after day we are losing more of what he was planning to say. This Work was a series of notations, for some grander, dreamt mural. I do wish that we had Uncle Oscar's writings here, and Praester's unshredded and sensible. Together, they might flare into so many of those trenches in my revision.

Without the whole Work, for instance, we will never know what Friend was really like, as a person, or whether she did love him, as he so desperate to be loved. We will never know what became of the Bog Ponies, or whether the Town Cryer was a man or a woman or something else, or what it was that the Eremite did to Mr. Lights, in those lonely, ultimate days on the valley floor.

What we have instead, left behind, is a quinquereme preparation, an itinerarium of travel out of Loss, a road diary a little more juvenile than most. Only one scrap of page remains; let us see quite how much of the rest I remember, from previous readings. It is puzzling; the rest of me is waning, planing down to the grain, but my mind stays as sharp as an ensis. I have not quite descended to the sty just yet.

I cannot be expected to remember quite everything, veritas;. The parts that I do remember I have put into order of pressure, as best I am able, and though I am no artist I have provided descriptions of the sketches and scenes that made a particular impression on my young mind. All stress is the Eremite's, not my own, though gaps may be in my own knowledge or in the record itself.

Firstly, the contexte. It seems that the Eremite was by now already on the long road out of Loss, the caprine, darting track plotted in the stars and the breadcrumbs of himself, trudging in the other direction all that time before. We may assume, from the notes that he made on each day of his journey, that he was finding it hard to delude himself any longer. His guilt at the death of Loss, at the dousing of an entire folk, is so impregnated throughout. Look at the records of his breath, recorded every evening. We can imagine that he did not sleep much. Nor was there any reason to hide these records; he was so assured of his companion's illiteracy that he knew that his crimes would never be understood. What were thirty-nine scratches, every single day, to a Friend?

The records of breath do confirm one of the Eremite's virtues; throughout his entire life, no matter his dimension, he was extremely regular in every faculty. Look to the tabulations and calculations throughout The Underweather Vanity. He has even boasted, in other Works, of cults marking their festivals by his toilet. And indeed, the breaths here do form a calendar; thirty nine, without fail, in and out, every single day.

However, as you all should have been taught, every calendar has its leap. There was one such energetic day, in Loss, a hot day when he was unsated by the cooking of the very worst cook, and decided to eat from a different sort of plate.

The Eremite has made no secret in the record that he wanted Friend, in that childish way that we have all come to recognise. I thought that I might have to puzzle it out, this calamity that murdered a public, but he was almost triumphant in his blatancy.

On the day that he removed his mask and kissed her, he registered only thirty breaths, in and out of his tubes.

Nine is not an important number, in any theology that I know of, but he would spend the rest of his time in Loss fixated upon those lost breaths, trying not to accuse himself. I feel well, we can imagine him telling himself, there is nothing wrong with me, as they moaned through the dirt all around him. He was always so excellent at imagining other outcomes and other blames. Perhaps he might have blamed Friend for distracting him, for making him lose count that day, for some atmospheric disturbance, the influence of spirits, anything, but his own want.

You can just imagine him, can't you, boygirls, on the road, checking her from temple to temple, looking for any sort of mantle, probing her mouth with those incredible fingers, hunting for cobblestones. He would even check her back, for the bruises that the rest of her people developed, evidence, he thought, of flagellation.

But, of course, he would find nothing on her, and so reinforce his delusion that he had not infected them. She did not wear a black crown, and so he enthroned her in other ways, certain of his innocence. If she was well, the only one who had smelt his breath in a hundred years, then he could not be accused.

But who was there still to accuse him?

The pigs, for all their lack of discernment, did teach me something as a girl. I often found them very gross, and would stand at the edge of the sty after school, shouting my subjunctives in and listening for the screams back.

Mummy and Daddy showed me, however, how the beasts could be used as a barometer. About five times in my infanthood a plague would pass through the herd, and they were loath to cull the whole lot. And so they would quietly walk amongst them, wearing homemade masks out of sack and snorkel, and make an inspection. They would ignore the sows turning out cold paste instead of milk, ignore the backfatters grinding their teeth into flour, ignore the boars corning each other in blind rages.

Before long, they would find the happiest pig, the one without sores or mucus, and lead it off to be shot, far from any watercourse.
