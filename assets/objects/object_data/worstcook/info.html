An Essay On Islandscript

From his own discoveries in the peat-bed of the Institute, it is easy to deduce that one of the Eremite's greatest Works, The Underweather Vanity, containing an account of his time in Loss, was completed primarily in Islandscript, a typographical philosophy which encompasses not only the lettering, kerning, and the layout of any one page, but out to the ordering, binding and presentation of the entire Work, and then up further, into the loft of the writer's own sensibilities and lifestyle. It is a House Style lived in completely, built around a man of exacting tastes and perfectionist practice.

Consider the mislaid incunabula. From what we know of its construction, it is extremely large and sturdy, build of solid mahogany planks, buoyant and resistant to rot, and in designation of size being perhaps Octuple Elephant. From the Eremite's own references, I would estimate that when stood on end, it stands much higher than a \\\\clerk////, perhaps five feet six in some calculations, and as wide across as a door. The pages within are, as any sufficiently sick \\_\clerk//__ will know, of varying qualities, weaves, pulps and compositions (owing to the Eremite's rather mongrel process of procuring raw materials) and were bound inside the incunabula with brass pins hammered flush into the wood.

Some pages span the entire height and width of the inculabula, and may more properly be called tapestries whereas others, such as the ones from which ____Clerk____ Chester-Stokes' transcript derives, are or were mere scraps, no wider across than a palm. I had the honour of studying this shard before it was destroyed, one of the many presented in quarto, a format of which, despite its rather bizarre appearance to this ____clerk____, was apparently a favourite of both the author and the transcriber.

If one were to imagine these pages as islands on a sea of wood, some the size of continents, others of atolls and reefs, then this metaphor extends down into the text itself; the Eremite very often grouped his thoughts in tight enclaves of words, the last descender providing a ferry to the next, and then the next, and the next, and the next. And, remember, now, poppets, remember the pins? Any \_\\clerk//__ may bend their knees, if there are not too turgid, and root about their quarren; there, one is almost guaranteed to uncover the gleam of bronze. Consider the pin, raise it to your eye; see the mica of the sea, the kink the waves put in. Even in the glue of his thoughts, the Eremite was considered marine. He considered all things islands, and lonely, to boot. This ____clerk____ sympathises.

I leave the above as an introduction to my tenure overseeing the typographical study of the Eremite's oeuvre; all queries should now be left in the soil for me, and faith taken that the worms will find me.
